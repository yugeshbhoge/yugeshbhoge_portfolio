Master's Thesis

Bayesian Formalism to Distinguish Kilonovae

Discriminating between Binary Neutron Star (BNS) and Neutron Star-Black Hole (NSBH) mergers using multi-band photometry.

Scientific Motivation

Since GW170817, the challenge in multimessenger astronomy has been distinguishing BNS from NSBH mergers, especially when the black hole falls into the "mass gap" or the system has high degeneracy in chirp mass. Gravitational waves alone often yield ambiguous parameter estimates. This research demonstrates that the electromagnetic counterpart, the Kilonova holds the key to breaking this degeneracy by analyzing the opacity-driven cooling rates of the ejecta.

Methodology

  • GW Inference: Utilized Bilby for Bayesian parameter estimation.
  • Waveform Models: IMRPhenomNSBH (NSBH) and IMRPhenomNRTidalV2 (BNS).
  • EM Modeling: Integrated the Arnett-Chatzoupoulos-Villar (ACV) semi-analytical model to simulate light curves.
  • Physics: Modeled the "blue" kilonova component driven by lanthanide-poor dynamical ejecta opacity \(\kappa\).

Key Finding

"Photometric bands B and u provide unambiguous discrimination between merger types over a 2-day timescale due to distinct cooling rates."

Applications

Allows for rapid classification of merger types within 48 hours of detection, prioritizing resources for spectroscopic follow-up.

Implications

Improving distinction aids in constraining the Neutron Star Equation of State (EoS) and understanding the yield of heavy r-process elements in the universe.

Theoretical Framework

The energy evolution equation in Arnett's model is given by

$$ \frac{dE}{dt} + p\frac{dV}{dt} = \dot{Q}_{decay} - L $$

The luminosity \(L(t)\) is given by,

$$L(t) = 2\,e^{-\left(\frac{t}{t_{\rm d}}\right)^2} \int_0^t \left(\frac{t'}{t_{\rm d}}\right) e^{\left(\frac{t'}{t_{\rm d}}\right)^2}\, \dot{E}(t')\,\frac{dt'}{t_{\rm d}}$$

The bolometric luminosity \(L(t)\) is powered by the radioactive decay of r-process nuclei. The heating rate \(\dot{\epsilon}(t)\) used in the simulation is modeled as:

$$ \dot{\epsilon}(t) = \epsilon_{0} M_{ej} \left[ \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{\pi} \arctan \left( \frac{t - t_0}{\sigma} \right) \right]^{\alpha} $$

Where \(\epsilon_0\) is the specific heating rate, \(M_{ej}\) is the ejecta mass, and \(\alpha \approx 1.3\) is the power-law decay index. The diffusion timescale \(t_d\), determining the peak brightness, scales with opacity \(\kappa\) (Here, \(\kappa = 211.98 \times e^{-12.33 \times Y_{e}}\) (Ekanger et al.), here \(Y_e\) is electron fraction):

$$ t_d \propto \left( \frac{\kappa M_{ej}}{v_{ej}} \right)^{1/2} $$
Blue KNe: Typically lower opacity \(\kappa < 1 cm^2/g\) \(\rightarrow\) higher \(Y_e\) lanthanide poor .
Red KNe: Often higher opacity due to lanthanide richness \(\rightarrow\) lower \(Y_e\).
