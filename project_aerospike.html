Altitude Compensation in Aerospike Engines

A CFD analysis comparing isentropic expansion efficiency of truncated aerospike nozzles versus conventional bell nozzles.

Motivation

Conventional bell nozzles are geometrically fixed, designed for a single ambient pressure ($P_a$). This leads to efficiency losses due to over-expansion at sea level (flow separation) and under-expansion in vacuum.

The Aerospike engine proposes a solution: an "inside-out" nozzle where the expansion surface is open to the atmosphere. This allows the exhaust plume to dynamically adjust to the changing $P_a$ during ascent, theoretically maintaining optimal $I_{sp}$ at all altitudes.

Theoretical Framework

1. General Thrust Equation

$$ F = \dot{m} V_e + (P_e - P_a) A_e $$

Significance: The pressure term $(P_e - P_a)A_e$ causes drag when $P_a > P_e$ (over-expansion). Aerospikes minimize this penalty by ensuring $P_e \approx P_a$.

2. Prandtl-Meyer Expansion

$$ \nu(M) = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma+1}{\gamma-1}} \tan^{-1} \dots $$

Significance: The flow turns around the cowl lip via expansion fans. In truncated spikes, a "base bleed" creates a recirculating fluid spike, adding a base pressure thrust component ($P_{base} A_{base}$).

CFD Methodology

  • Solver: ANSYS Fluent (Density-based, Implicit, 2D Axisymmetric).
  • Turbulence: $k-\epsilon$ standard model for high-speed compressible flow robustness.
  • Geometry: Truncated linear aerospike (20% truncation) vs. standard Bell nozzle.

Results: Performance Comparison

Comparing Thrust Coefficient ($C_f$) across altitude regimes.

Analysis

The data confirms the altitude compensation effect. At Sea Level (0 km), Aerospike maintains near-optimal expansion to match the full atmospheric performance of the Bell nozzle. At high altitudes (>10000 feet), the Bell cone type nozzle suffers from over-expansion (lower $C_f$), whereas the truncated base pressure contributes to thrust, allowing Aerospike to maintain it's efficiency at higher altitudes. Also as altitude increases one can see a clear decline in PMF for Aerospike engine. $$\textbf{Low PMF} \rightarrow \textbf{Lesser fuel needed to move the mass} \rightarrow \textbf{higher efficieny}$$

Applications

SSTO (Single-Stage-To-Orbit): Vehicles like the conceptual X-33/VentureStar require engines efficient at both sea level (liftoff) and vacuum (orbit insertion) without staging. The Aerospike is the primary candidate for such mission profiles.

Implications

Mass Reduction: By truncating the spike at ~20% of its theoretical length, we reduce engine weight significantly. The "base pressure" generated by the recirculation zone compensates for the missing physical structure, optimizing the thrust-to-weight ratio.
